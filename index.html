Transcript

ADAM: Rosie, I’ve been thinking about our presentation on nutrition and obesity...

ROSIE: Me too. What about your experiment on artificial sweeteners?

ADAM: It went well. I used a blind test - the subjects didn’t know which drink had the sweetener and which had sugar. And I made sure they each did the test three times to ensure the results were consistent.

ROSIE: My nut experiment was harder. I think the main problem was I didn't grind them into a fine enough powder, so the chemical didn't extract all the fat. And also, the scales I was using were only accurate to the nearest gram - I needed something much more precise.

ADAM: Interesting. I read a study about restaurant menus. It suggested that if you put the healthiest, low-calorie options at the very beginning of the menu, people are much more likely to choose them. It's all about how you organise the information.

ROSIE: True. But food labels are a nightmare. We agreed that manufacturers often make it nearly impossible to calculate the actual calorie count, like giving figures for 100g when the pack is 145g.

ROSIE: And exercise! Studies show that most people in England claim they are active, but they significantly overestimate the amount of exercise they actually do.

ADAM: It's like that train station design where they made the stairs wider and more central than the escalator. It naturally nudged people to take the stairs without even thinking about it. Simple physical changes like that can really change behaviour.

ROSIE: Should we include that? ADAM: I'm not sure. Let's ask our tutor Dr. Green what she thinks.

ROSIE: Okay. What's next? ADAM: Well, before we do anything else, we really need to sit down and map out exactly what's going in and the overall structure. We need a clear plan.
